## What Is a Prediction Market? {#what-is}

A prediction market is a trading platform where participants buy and sell shares representing the probability of a specific event occurring.

The core mechanic is simple:
- **YES shares** pay $1 if the event happens, $0 if it doesn't
- **NO shares** pay $1 if the event doesn't happen, $0 if it does
- The current price reflects the market's best estimate of the probability

If "Will the Fed cut rates in March?" is trading at YES = $0.68, the market is saying there's a 68% probability of a March rate cut.

That's not just a bet—it's a real-time probability estimate generated by thousands of people staking real money on their beliefs.

## How Do Prediction Markets Work? {#how-work}

### The Basic Mechanism

Every prediction market follows the same basic structure:

1. **A question is asked** with a clear, verifiable resolution condition
   - Example: "Will the S&P 500 close above 5,500 on March 31, 2026?"
2. **YES and NO shares are created**, each worth $1 at resolution
3. **Traders buy and sell** these shares based on their probability estimates
4. **Prices adjust continuously** as new information enters the market
5. **At resolution**, YES holders receive $1 (if event occurred) or $0 (if it didn't)

### The Role of the Market Maker

In traditional finance, market makers provide liquidity by continuously quoting both buy and sell prices. In prediction markets, this works differently:

- **Decentralized markets (like Polymarket)**: Automated market makers (AMMs) use liquidity pools to always offer prices, with spreads that widen as liquidity decreases
- **Centralized markets (like Kalshi)**: Traditional order book matching, similar to a stock exchange

In both cases, the **spread**—the difference between buy and sell prices—is the primary cost of trading.

### How Prices Reflect Probability

This is the most important concept: prediction market prices are probability estimates.

A market trading at YES = $0.72 means the collective judgment of all traders—weighted by their financial stakes—puts the probability at 72%.

This makes prediction markets uniquely valuable for several reasons:
- Prices update in real time as news breaks
- Traders have financial incentive to be accurate (unlike polls)
- The aggregation of diverse views reduces individual bias

## Why Prediction Markets Beat Traditional Forecasting {#why-better}

Prediction markets consistently outperform expert forecasters, polling averages, and institutional models. Here's why:

### Skin in the Game

When you stake money on a prediction, you're incentivized to be right. Pundit forecasts and polling averages have no financial accountability—prediction markets do.

### Real-Time Price Updates

News breaks. Markets adjust within minutes. A forecaster's quarterly report can't update in real time when a surprise jobs number drops. Prediction markets can.

### Aggregation of Diverse Information

No single forecaster has access to all relevant information. Markets aggregate signals from thousands of participants, each with different expertise and information sets.

### The Track Record

- Prediction markets accurately predicted the 2016 Brexit vote outcome when polls showed a narrow Remain lead
- During COVID, they provided more accurate vaccine timeline estimates than most expert consensus
- In the 2024 US election cycle, Polymarket tracked significantly more accurately than national polling averages throughout the campaign

## Prediction Markets and US Law {#legality}

This is the most common question, and the answer requires nuance.

### The Short Answer

**For US residents:** Kalshi is the only major regulated prediction market. Polymarket is not accessible to US users.

### The Full Picture

Prediction markets exist in a complex regulatory space in the United States. The CFTC (Commodity Futures Trading Commission) has jurisdiction over event contracts—which is what prediction market contracts are.

For most of its history, the CFTC took the position that event contracts tied to political outcomes were contrary to the public interest and couldn't be listed. This created a regulatory vacuum that drove most prediction market activity offshore (Polymarket) or into legal grey zones (PredictIt's no-action letter).

That changed with Kalshi. After a multi-year regulatory battle, Kalshi became the first entity to receive CFTC approval to list political event contracts as a Designated Contract Market—the same legal structure as the Chicago Mercantile Exchange.

**What this means:**
- **Kalshi:** CFTC-regulated, fully legal for US users, client funds segregated
- **Polymarket:** Decentralized, blocks US IP addresses, violates ToS for US residents
- **Manifold:** Free play-money only, no regulatory issues
- **PredictIt:** Operating under a no-action letter with $850 position limits and scope limited to politics

### Outside the US

In most jurisdictions outside the US, prediction markets operate in a less restricted environment. Polymarket serves users globally (excluding the US) and is the dominant platform by volume.

**For Polymarket:**

3. Purchase USDC from an exchange (Coinbase, Kraken, etc.)
4. Deposit USDC to your Polymarket account

### Step 3: Understand the Market

Before placing a trade, check:
- **Resolution date and conditions** — when does this market resolve, and exactly what counts as YES?
- **Liquidity** — how wide is the spread? For a $0.65 YES market, is the ask $0.65 or $0.70?
- **Market history** — how has the price moved over time?

### Step 4: Place Your Trade

Prediction markets work like simple stock trades:
- **Buying YES shares:** You're betting the event will happen
- **Buying NO shares:** You're betting the event won't happen
- **Position size:** The amount you invest equals the number of shares × the price per share
- **Maximum gain:** (1 - price paid) × shares for YES; price paid × shares for NO

**Example:**
You buy 100 YES shares at $0.65 on "Fed cuts rates in March"
- Cost: $65
- If YES: You receive $100 (profit: $35)
- If NO: You receive $0 (loss: $65)
- Implied odds: 65% probability of YES

### Step 5: Manage Your Position

- Prediction markets allow you to close positions early by selling your shares
- If you bought YES at $0.65 and the price moves to $0.80, you can sell for a profit without waiting for resolution
- Monitor markets as news develops—major news events can move prices dramatically

## How to Find the Best Odds {#best-odds}

Here's something most prediction market traders miss: **the same event often trades at different odds on different platforms simultaneously.**

On any given day, you might see:

## Prediction Market Strategies {#strategies}

### 1. Fade the Narrative

Prediction markets are influenced by media cycles. When a story dominates the news, it often inflates the probability of the event being discussed.

**Example:** An election poll shows a +5% lead for one candidate. Media coverage spikes. Prediction market prices overreact to the polling narrative. A savvy trader who knows polling averages historically move back toward equilibrium can fade the overreaction.

### 2. Resolution Arbitrage

Different platforms sometimes resolve identical events with slightly different criteria. Understanding exactly how each platform's resolution works can reveal pricing discrepancies.

### 3. Late-Breaking Information

Prediction market prices update continuously, but not instantly. In the minutes after a major data release, there's often a window where informed traders can move faster than the market consensus.

**Key events to watch:**
- Fed rate decisions (Kalshi/Polymarket both list Fed markets)
- Economic data releases (CPI, jobs reports)
- Election night vote counting

### 4. Time Value of Money

A market at YES = $0.80 with 60 days to resolution implies an annualized return of ~15% if it resolves YES. Markets that are near-certain (80-95%) with long time horizons can offer risk-adjusted returns comparable to fixed income.

### 5. Cross-Platform Arbitrage

When the same event trades at materially different prices on two platforms, a true arbitrage exists: buy YES on the cheaper platform, buy NO on the more expensive platform. If the spread exceeds transaction costs and you can execute on both, you lock in a risk-free profit.

**Practical note:** True arbitrage opportunities are rare and close quickly. More common is a directional preference—one platform is simply cheaper for the position you want.

## Risks and Limitations {#risks}

Prediction markets are a powerful tool, but not without risks.

### Liquidity Risk

Smaller markets can have wide spreads (5-15%) that make entry and exit expensive. Always check the spread before trading.

### Resolution Risk

Prediction markets resolve based on specific criteria. Understanding exactly what counts as YES matters. Edge cases happen: a market might resolve in a way that surprises traders based on strict interpretation of the resolution rules.

### Platform Risk

- **Decentralized platforms (Polymarket):** Smart contract bugs, though rare, represent a technical risk. No regulatory recourse if something goes wrong.
- **Centralized platforms (Kalshi):** Standard counterparty risk, mitigated by CFTC regulation and fund segregation.

### Regulatory Risk

The regulatory environment for prediction markets continues to evolve. Platforms can be forced to change operations or geo-restrict users on short notice.

### Concentration Risk

All trading markets carry concentration risk. Sizing positions appropriately and not over-concentrating in a single outcome is basic risk management.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

**What is a prediction market in simple terms?**
A prediction market lets you bet money on whether a specific event will happen. If you're right, you make money. The market price tells you the probability that traders collectively assign to that event.

**Are prediction markets legal in the US?**
Kalshi is the only major prediction market that is fully legal for US users. It's regulated by the CFTC. Polymarket is not accessible to US residents. Manifold uses play-money and has no restrictions.

**What's the difference between a prediction market and a betting market?**
Practically, they're similar: you stake money on an outcome. The key differences are in structure and regulation. Prediction markets typically use a continuous trading mechanism (like stocks), while sports betting uses fixed odds set by a bookmaker. In the US, prediction markets regulated by the CFTC are classified as commodity contracts, not gambling.

**Can you make money on prediction markets?**
Yes—experienced traders do. But it requires accurate probability estimation, discipline around position sizing, and attention to market mechanics (spreads, liquidity, resolution criteria, fees). Most retail participants underperform because they pay too much in fees or over-size individual positions. Trading on a platform with 0% fees (like MoonX) directly improves your net returns compared to platforms that charge 1-10%.

**Which prediction market has the best odds?**
It depends on the specific event and the timing. Because platforms operate independently, the same event often prices differently. Checking multiple platforms—or using an aggregator like MoonX—before trading is the best way to find the optimal entry point.

**How are prediction market prices determined?**
Prices are set by supply and demand. When more traders want to buy YES than NO, YES prices rise. When more traders want to sell YES or buy NO, prices fall. In aggregate, this process produces probability estimates that are remarkably accurate.

**What is the largest prediction market?**
Polymarket is the largest by trading volume, processing $500M+ in monthly volume as of 2026. Kalshi is the largest regulated platform in the US.
